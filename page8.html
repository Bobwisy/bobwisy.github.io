Labour Relations & Your Business

The South African labour market is one laden with weaves at many corners; both employers and employees alike must be careful when navigating this terrain. 

Thanks for filling out the form!
There are a number of laws in South Africa that apply to the labour market, such laws include the Labour Relations Act 66 of 1995 (as amended), the Basic Conditions of Employment Act 75 of 1997 (as amended), the Employment Equity Act 55 of 1998 (as amended) and a number of other laws and regulations attached to these laws.
In navigating the waves of the South African labour market, some keys points to keep in mind are:
• Employers should have properly drafted documentations,
• Employers should have proper processes and procedures in place,
• External counsel should be consulted when a dispute arises, and
• Employers and Employees alike should be mindful of reputational damage.
Depending on the size of the company, some of the documentations an employer should have in place are properly drafted and executed employment contracts, code of conduct or conditions of employment, disciplinary procedures, internal processes and procedures for a range of issues. These documents should be professionally drafted in line with the nature and size of the business, circulated internally and internal training should be conducted. These documents should also be updated often to accommodate ever-changing laws as well as nature and size of the business.
Considering that employers have more requirements and obligations mandated by the labour laws, and have more to loose, so to speak, it is essential that they have processes and procedures in place to protect their interests. 
Depending on the size of the business, it may not be sufficient to have a human
 resources employee on hand to deal with labour related matters, it may be necessary to consult with external counsel from time to time. Sometimes, it’s a question of the internal personnel being too close to the situation to follow the process and keep to the requirements of applicable laws, therefore an objective opinion will sometimes save the company time and money in the long run.
It is a reality that disputes arise in employer/employee relations and this will have to be resolved either internally (with or without the input of an external party) or externally (by the CCMA or otherwise). It is generally in the best interests of all parties if an amicable solution is reached but in most cases, it takes the involvement of an external party for a situation to be resolved.
Should a dispute arise to the level of the CCMA being involved, an employer should definitely consult with a labour relations specialist for advice on the situation at hand.
One thing to keep in mind with all labour relations issues (for both employer and employee) is reputational damage that may be done in dealing with these issues. Therefore care must be taken and every situation thought through appropriately to maintain a person or company’s reputation constantly.
