Can A Comma Ruin It All?

A court in Maine, U.S.A. recently adjudicated a case where a comma or lack thereof in legislation could cost a company $10 million in relation to overtime pay.

Thanks for filling out the form!
The truck drivers who brought the suit won because the court decided that the absence of a comma caused sufficient uncertainty to warrant a ruling in their favour.
The argument over punctuation marks, especially a comma is usually a pedantic one and most times is neither here nor there but when there’s money involved, parties will do well to ensure that not even a comma can cause them financial loss. Hence, a professional should be used to draft or review a contract or other legal document.
With the growth of the internet, a growing number of people and businesses are relying on generic contracts downloaded from the internet or contracts received from a friend that may look like what they intend to use it for. Such contracts are generally used in the hope that nothing goes wrong.
The problem with such generic or ‘borrowed’ contracts is that it mostly does not take into account the user’s particular situation or interests and this may prove to be very costly should a problem or dispute arise.
There are essential elements that parties need to interrogate before using a contract and these would differ from matter to matter. An example could be the correct and complete identity of parties. If there is a company that is a party to the contract, the company’s name, registration number and address should be reflected; and the name and identity number of the company’s representative should ideally be captured as well.


Clients sometimes lean towards these downloaded or ‘borrowed’ contracts because they want short contracts and are concerned with the length of professionally drafted contracts; but they fail to recognize that the length of the contract is dependent on the type and nature of the matter. Obviously there are some matters that can do without the excessively long contract but all in all, the length should match the nature and complexity of the matter. Shorter contracts do not necessarily translate to simpler contracts mainly because they leave matters unattended and open to interpretation.
Despite best drafting efforts, no professional can guarantee that a contract they have drafted is beyond reproach but they can prepare the contract in a way that significantly limits the client’s risk. Paying attention to grammar and details.
It is not just important to have a professional draft your contracts, it is good business practice to regularly have your contracts updated or audited as the status of your business may have evolved and legislation may have changed over the years. This process might save you from the pitfalls of unwanted comma issues and reduce your financial risks related to those contracts.
