Transacting Across Borders

Cross-border transactions involve persons or entities doing business with each other or one another across borders or boundaries. In essence, at least one of the parties must be based in a different jurisdiction (country) from where the transaction is originating or being concluded.

Such transactions generally involve laws and regulations from one or more jurisdictions and this must be sufficiently taken into account from the inception to finalisation of the transaction.
Two crucial points to keep in mind for such transactions are:
• Applicable laws and regulations, and
• Business and social culture of the applicable jurisdictions.
The laws and regulations applicable will depend on the type and complexity of the transaction but some laws that are often involved are those relating to tax, companies, fraud or anti-bribery, local content or labour relations. These laws should be considered especially when contracts are being prepared.
Over and above applicable national laws and regulations of the applicable jurisdiction, there are two laws from the United States of America (USA) and the United Kingdom (UK) respectively that should be taken into account whether or not the transaction involves these countries. The laws in question are The Foreign Corrupt Practices Act of 1977, as amended, 15 U.S.C. §§ 78dd-1, et seq. (“FCPA”) and The Bribery Act 2010 (c.23). 
These legislations aim to curb bribery and corruption in transactions involving person or institutions from their respective countries, therefore they are to be taken into account in such situations. Even with transactions not involving 
persons or entities from the USA or the UK, it is generally regarding good corporate practice to apply them to cross-border transactions.
In addition to taking into account regulations and laws, it is pertinent for parties to take into considering the business and social cultures of the jurisdictions they are transacting in because this could make or break a transaction. In some cultures, declining to have lunch may be the end of a transaction, leaving the other party(ies) wondering what went wrong. 
In any event, cross-border transactions are growing in leaps and bounds, especially within Africa. It is no longer just individuals or institutions from the West entering and concluding transactions with counterparts on the African continent, but Africans are increasingly trading and transacting with one another across borders. Being a fellow African though does not prepare Africans for the intricacies of cross-border transactions and one would do well to consult a business advisor before embarking on a cross-border transaction.
