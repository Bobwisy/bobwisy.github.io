The Importance of Corporate Governance In A Business

Corporate governance has been a part of doing business in the West for several years and has gained notoriety in South Africa in recent years but is still seriously lagging behind in the rest of Africa.

South Africa has tailed the United Kingdom (UK) on corporate governance and some may say that as the UK goes in this regard, so does South Africa.
Some principles of corporate governance are embedded in the South African Companies Act, 71 of 2008 (as amended) especially Part F, taking note particularly of sections 77 and 78 which cover conduct and liability of directors. All in all, the Act requires that directors objectively act:
• in good faith,
• in the best interests of the company, and with the degree of care, skill and diligence worthy of the position.  
Over and above the requirements of the Companies Act, South Africa has the Kings Code, which is now in its fourth review. King IV code on corporate governance (King IV™ or King Code) provides 17 principles to be taken into account by organisations or institutions (entities). Some of these principles are relevant to entities in varying degrees and in some cases not at all. For an entity listed on the Johannesburg Stock Exchange (JSE), compliance with the King Code is required but not so for small to medium sized businesses or unlisted entities.

Although corporate governance benefits both internal and external operations, it is arguable that the greater interaction an entity has with external parties, whether customers or investors, the greater attention it should pay to corporate governance.
Irrespective of the type of organization; private, public or institutional, corporate governance should be considered a key aspect of doing business. At the very least, it builds investor confidence and boosts an entity’s reputation.
In recent times, the South African public sector has seen a ‘break down’ in corporate governance with the public sector and there are calls for this to be rectified with immediate effect. Appointing the right board of directors, conducting in-house corporate governance trainings and adherence to corporate governance principles will go a long way to fixing the governance issues rife within the affected government institutions.
Entities should consult with a corporate governance consultant to be updated as the current workings of corporate governance, especially as applicable to their business.
